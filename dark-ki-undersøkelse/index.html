What the top-right cell actually contains

Across the 30 people in this group, the dominant pattern is ChatGPT free or personal subscription, used for design narratives, client emails, and project research. By the AI Privacy Guide's four-level classification, this is Level 3 data flowing into Level 1 tools. Several are senior architects with direct client responsibility. This is not the awareness problem people often assume it is: most know AI exists. They do not know which version of the tool they should be using or that the tier matters.

Why Zinc stands out

Zinc Interior is the only studio where firm-tier subscription penetration exceeds free-tier use. Four of nine respondents work through the Microsoft 365 subscription, two of them in administrative roles where Copilot inside Teams and Outlook is the natural entry point. This is the closest pattern in the survey to the firm's stated policy direction. Whatever access or onboarding produced this result for Zinc is worth understanding and replicating.

Where to focus

Six things to flag from this round

Synthesis of new patterns, things that have shifted since the first 28 responses, and items worth bringing to leadership.

1

The free-tier crisis is not closing on its own

Across the larger sample, the pattern has hardened: 59% are now putting project-sensitive material into free or personal-tier tools, up from a similar proportion at n=28. Policy without access closure will lose to convenience. Either Copilot Studio needs to handle the actual tasks people are doing (design narratives, summaries, translation), or the firm needs to procure a defensible alternative tier (ChatGPT Team, Claude Team) for the use cases where Copilot falls short.

2

Sustainability is now a coalition, not a single voice

The previous survey had one principled abstainer raising energy concerns. This round has three voices (Ivar, Britt, and one anonymous respondent) explicitly asking for an organisational conversation about when AI use is unnecessary, what tools are energy-intensive, and the ethics of country-of-origin. This is no longer fringe. The firm needs a public position on responsible use, not just safe use. Sidrah Hassan's ethics framing and the KI-manifest give Dark the credibility to lead this.

3

Seniors are the lowest-confidence group and the highest-risk one

Senior designers and architects score 2.18 on confidence, lower than juniors and project managers, despite handling the most client-sensitive work. The mentorship line is running backwards: juniors are confident, seniors are not. Dark Academy needs a senior-targeted track that does not feel remedial. The opening framing should respect their expertise and offer something specifically useful for client-facing seniors.

4

Lark Landskap needs a dedicated intervention

Five respondents, lowest confidence in the firm at 2.20, 100% on free tier or personal subscriptions, and Nina's explicit request for firm-paid licenses. Lark joined the survey this round, so this is the first read on them, but the signal is clear. A short, Lark-focused session on tool access and data handling, possibly piggybacking on Zinc's working model, would address the issue faster than waiting for firm-wide rollout.

5

The product backlog is writing itself

Three concrete user-driven product requests surface again: a regulations agent for TEK17, Byggforsk, and universal-design guidance (Therese); an internal project-search RAG covering drawings, presentations, and descriptions (Jennifer); and AI-assisted rendering from ArchiCAD exports (anonymous Stavanger–Bergen senior). The first two fit cleanly inside the planned SharePoint + Microsoft Graph + Azure AI Search architecture. The third needs an approved-environment design before it can move forward.

6

Two genuinely confused respondents deserve their own onboarding path

Jon (#42) asks how to log in to AI tools at all. Ingrid (#46) is unclear on the difference between licenses, free versions, and copyright. These are not the same problem as confused power users. There is an audience here that needs "Start here" content before any policy training will land. A 15-minute onboarding video for people at zero, sitting before the Week 1 Dark Academy course, would catch them.

What I would recommend reporting to leadership

The honest version of this report is that DDG already has an AI program. It just is not the one the firm chose. ChatGPT free, run by individual staff with no contractual data protection, is the de facto firm AI. The privacy guide is the right response, but it needs to land with concrete tool access changes alongside it, otherwise it becomes paperwork that documents a risk rather than removes it. The fastest single action that would change the numbers in this dashboard is procuring ChatGPT Team (or Claude Team) for the people already using ChatGPT daily, while Copilot Studio continues to be developed as the longer-term internal direction.

Methodology and caveats

A note on reading this

Fifty-one responses across six studios is enough to surface clear patterns but not enough to do formal statistics. Percentages should be read as directional rather than precise. The signal is strong because patterns are large and the multiple-choice answers converge with the open comments, but slicing by individual studio (particularly Fredrikstad at n=4 and HK at n=5) should be treated cautiously.

The survey was opt-in. People with stronger feelings about AI in either direction are likely overrepresented relative to the silent middle of the firm. The two principled abstainers and the three sustainability raisers are a real signal, but the firm-wide proportion of people holding those views is probably higher than the survey shows, not lower.

The next survey, run six months after the privacy guide and tool access changes have been in effect, will tell us whether anything moved. The right metrics to track between now and then: share of respondents on firm-tier tools, mean confidence rating, and proportion answering that they put sensitive materials into free tools.
